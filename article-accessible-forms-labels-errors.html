Accessible Forms: Labels, Errors, and Instructions

Accessible forms help people understand what is being asked, what went wrong, and how to fix it without guessing or losing their place.

Labels Should Be Persistent and Programmatic

Every input needs a visible label connected to the field. Placeholder text is not enough because it disappears during entry and may not provide a reliable accessible name.

  • Use a connected <label> for text fields, selects, checkboxes, and radio controls.
  • Group related options with <fieldset> and <legend>.
  • Keep required field guidance available before submission.

Real Example: Payment Error

Instead of "Invalid input," a useful error says "Enter the card expiration date in MM/YY format." The message should be connected to the field and announced when the user reaches it.

Error Summaries Help Users Recover

For long forms, place an error summary near the top after submission. Each summary item should link to the field that needs attention so keyboard and screen reader users can move directly to the problem.
